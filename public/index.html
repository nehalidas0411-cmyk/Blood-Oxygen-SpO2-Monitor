About the Project

Why This Matters

Blood oxygen saturation (SpO2) is a critical physiological parameter. A value below 95% can indicate respiratory distress, cardiac conditions, or altitude sickness — often before symptoms become visible.

This project implements a complete photoplethysmography (PPG) pipeline using the MAX30102 sensor module — from raw optical data acquisition over I²C, through digital signal processing on an Arduino Nano, to real-time output on an OLED screen.

The focus was on system integration and software DSP: mastering I²C bus sharing, extracting AC/DC signal components from raw samples, and applying the ratio-of-ratios algorithm for a calibrated SpO2 reading.

Working Principle

How It Works

Two wavelengths of light pass through the fingertip — oxygenated and deoxygenated haemoglobin absorb them differently, revealing SpO2.

01

Dual LED Emission

MAX30102's internal Red (660nm) and IR (880nm) LEDs pulse alternately through the fingertip under the chip's internal timing control.

02

Photodetection

An integrated photodetector captures transmitted light intensity. The chip's AFE digitises both channels and sends 18-bit samples over I²C to the Arduino.

03

Signal Processing

The Arduino reads samples, applies moving-average and median filters, and separates the pulsatile AC component from the DC tissue baseline.

04

R-ratio → SpO2

R = (AC_red/DC_red) ÷ (AC_IR/DC_IR) maps to SpO2 % via calibration. BPM is extracted from inter-peak intervals and displayed on the OLED.

Hardware Stack

Key Components

MAX30102 Pulse Oximeter Sensor

Integrated dual-wavelength (660nm Red + 880nm IR) PPG front-end with 18-bit ADC. Communicates over I²C at address 0x57. Operates at 3.3V and includes internal ambient light cancellation.

Sensor IC — U1

Arduino Nano (ATmega328P)

8-bit AVR microcontroller running the firmware. Pulls SpO2 and BPM data from the MAX30102 over the I²C bus (A4/A5), applies DSP filtering, computes the R-ratio, and drives the OLED display.

MCU — 16 MHz

0.96" OLED Display (SSD1306)

128×64 monochrome I²C display on a 4-pin header (VCC, GND, SCL, SDA). Renders SpO2 %, BPM, and a live pleth waveform. Shares the I²C bus with the MAX30102 at its own device address (0x3C).

Output Display — U2

Breadboard

Solderless breadboard for rapid prototyping. Allows easy reconfiguration of connections between the sensor, Arduino Nano, and OLED display without soldering.

Prototyping Platform

Jumper Wires

Male-to-male and male-to-female jumper wires connecting all modules. Four critical lines form the shared I²C bus: VCC (3.3V), GND, SCL, and SDA shared by the MAX30102 and OLED.

Engineering Challenges

Problems Solved

// I²C Bus Sharing

Both the MAX30102 (0x57) and the SSD1306 OLED (0x3C) share a single I²C bus. The correct 4.7 kΩ pull-up resistors (R1, R2) to 3.3V were critical — incorrect values caused bus contention and corrupted ADC reads.

// Ambient Light Rejection

Room light contaminated the photodetector and caused erratic SpO2 values. The MAX30102's ambient light cancellation register was configured, and sampling was synchronised to LED-on intervals only.

// AC/DC Signal Separation

The pulsatile AC component (cardiac pulse) is much smaller than the static DC tissue absorption baseline. A moving-average subtraction algorithm in firmware isolated the AC waveform needed for the R-ratio.

// Stable SpO2 Readout

Raw R-ratio values are noisy sample-to-sample. A sliding-window median filter over the last 10 R values was applied before the SpO2 lookup, preventing erratic jumps in the displayed reading.
